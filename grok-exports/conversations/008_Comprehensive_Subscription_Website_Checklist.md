## Message 10 - ASSISTANT

Neurodiversity in UX design refers to creating digital interfaces that accommodate a wide range of neurological differences — including ADHD, autism, dyslexia, dyscalculia, and more — rather than treating them as edge cases. The goal is **inclusive, flexible experiences** that reduce cognitive load, sensory overload, and frustration while benefiting everyone (e.g., clearer layouts help all users).

This approach builds on WCAG guidelines but goes further by emphasizing **user agency**, predictability, and empathy. Recent resources (2024–2025) highlight that neuro-inclusive design is becoming synonymous with good design overall.

### Core Principles of Neuro-Inclusive UX Design
Here are the most commonly recommended principles, synthesized from expert sources:

1. **Customizable Interfaces** — Let users control font size, spacing, color schemes (e.g., dark/calm modes), contrast, animation reduction, and sound/feedback levels. This empowers neurodivergent users to tailor the experience to their needs.
2. **Clear, Consistent, and Predictable Layouts** — Use standard navigation patterns, persistent UI elements, left-aligned text, and consistent labeling. Avoid surprises like auto-playing media or sudden layout shifts.
3. **Reduce Cognitive & Sensory Overload** — Employ progressive disclosure (reveal info gradually), ample white space, minimal distractions (no flashing/rapid animations), and limit choices per screen.
4. **Simple, Literal Language** — Prefer plain, direct wording; avoid idioms, jargon, metaphors, or clever microcopy. Use short sentences and clear instructions/error messages.
5. **Flexible Interaction Patterns** — Offer multiple ways to complete tasks (e.g., keyboard, voice, mouse). Support "error forgiveness" with easy undo/recovery and autosave for long forms.
6. **Focus on Readability & Processing** — Dyslexia-friendly fonts (e.g., OpenDyslexic or sans-serif with good spacing), sufficient line height, and structured content (headings, bullet points) help with text processing.
7. **User Control & Autonomy** — Include toggles for "focus/calm mode," reduced motion (via prefers-reduced-motion), and options to hide non-essential elements.

These principles often align with cognitive accessibility efforts like COGA (Cognitive Accessibility) and benefit neurotypical users too (e.g., better focus in noisy environments).
