### Limited Partnership (LP) vs. Multi-Member LLC in **Colorado** for SSDI Protection

Colorado-specific rules make both structures low-cost and similar in ongoing fees, but the core differences in **SSDI protection**, passivity, and management remain the same as nationally. As of December 31, 2025, here's the updated comparison focused on your state.

#### Costs in Colorado: Virtually Identical (Both ~$25/year Periodic Report)
Colorado charges a flat **$25 Periodic Report fee** annually for all reporting entities, including both multi-member LLCs and limited partnerships (LPs). This fee increased from $10 to $25 starting July 1, 2024, and applies uniformly.

- **Multi-Member LLC** → Single entity → $25/year Periodic Report.
- **Limited Partnership (LP)** → Also a single reporting entity → $25/year Periodic Report (no separate fee for a GP entity, even if you use an LLC as general partner for liability protection—the GP LLC would need its own $25 if it's a separate LLC, but many setups avoid that by having an individual or the same entity structure).

Unlike states with higher or doubled fees for LPs (e.g., due to dual filings), Colorado keeps it simple and affordable for both. Your original $264 vs. $528 likely referenced a different state or outdated info—here, cost is **not** a differentiator.

Formation fees are also similar:
- LLC Articles of Organization: $50 online.
- LP Certificate of Limited Partnership: $50 online.

#### SSDI Protection: LP Still Offers Stronger Built-In Passive Classification
The federal/IRS/SSA rules on passivity dominate, and Colorado follows standard uniform acts (Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act):
- **LP Advantage** — Limited partners are structurally passive by law. You cannot manage or control the business without risking reclassification as a general partner. This inherent restriction provides the clearest evidence of a passive role for SSDI purposes—income is typically non-SGA investment income, not self-employment earnings.
- **Multi-Member LLC** — Flexibility allows passive members, but you must explicitly document it in the operating agreement (e.g., non-managing member with no control). SSA/IRS may scrutinize more closely if involvement creeps in, as LLC members aren't automatically passive.

If your #1 goal is **absolute strongest SSDI protection** with minimal risk of active classification, **LP** remains superior due to its enforced passivity.

#### Management, Perception, and Other Factors
- **Simpler Management & More Modern** → Multi-member LLC wins. Flexible operating agreements, no required GP/LP divide, easier to explain (LLCs are far more common today).
- **Passive Documentation** → LLC requires stronger operating agreement language; LP's structure does much of the work for you.
- **Tax Deductions/Benefits** → Identical pass-through treatment (Form 1065/K-1) for both in Colorado—no difference in write-offs.

#### Colorado-Specific Recommendation
- **Choose Limited Partnership (LP) If**:
  - Absolute strongest SSDI protection via built-in passivity is priority.
  - You want the clearest "hands-off" classification without heavy reliance on documentation.
  - Cost/complexity difference is negligible here.

- **Choose Multi-Member LLC If**:
  - You prefer simplicity, flexibility, and a modern structure.
  - Easier management and explanation outweigh the slightly higher (but still low) scrutiny risk for passivity.
  - You're comfortable with a strong operating agreement documenting your passive role.

Both work well for truly passive income in Colorado, with the same low $25 annual fee and identical tax perks. SSDI rules are federal, so state entity choice affects evidence of passivity more than anything. Always report income to SSA and consult a disability attorney or tax advisor familiar with SSDI/self-employment for your exact setup—small details (e.g., any services provided) can matter. This isn't legal advice!

### Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction – Section 199A (2025 Rules)

The **Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction** allows eligible pass-through business owners (sole proprietors, partnerships, multi-member LLCs taxed as partnerships, S corps) to deduct **up to 20%** of their qualified business income on personal returns. #### Basic Calculation (2025)
- **Tentative Deduction**: 20% of QBI (net profit from qualified trade/business, after deductions but before QBI).
- **Overall Limit**: Lesser of tentative deduction or 20% of taxable income (minus net capital gains).
- QBI excludes wages, guaranteed payments, investment income.

#### 2025 Income Thresholds & Phase-Outs
| Filing Status          | Full Deduction Below | Phase-In Range (Limitations Apply) | Full Phase-Out Above |
|------------------------|----------------------|------------------------------------|----------------------|

- **Below Threshold**: Full 20% (no wage/UBIA limits; SSTBs treated normally).
- **In Phase-In**: Partial limits (wage/property caps phased in; SSTB income phased out).

### QBI Safe Harbor for Rental Real Estate (Revenue Procedure 2019-38) – 2025 Details

The **QBI safe harbor** for rental real estate, outlined in **IRS Revenue Procedure 2019-38** (finalized from Notice 2019-07), remains unchanged and fully applicable for 2025 tax filings. This optional safe harbor allows a **Rental Real Estate Enterprise (RREE)** to be automatically treated as a **trade or business** solely for purposes of the Section 199A Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction (up to 20% of net qualified income).

If met, your rental activity qualifies for QBI without needing to prove it rises to a Section 162 trade or business under general case law (which is fact-specific and riskier). Failing the safe harbor doesn't disqualify you—you can still claim QBI if facts/circumstances support a trade or business.

#### Definition of Rental Real Estate Enterprise (RREE)
- An interest in real property held for the production of rents (may include single or multiple properties).
- Owned directly by the taxpayer or through a disregarded entity (e.g., single-member LLC).
- Commercial and residential properties **cannot** be combined in the same RREE.
- You can group similar properties (e.g., all residential) into one RREE to ease meeting requirements.
- Once grouped, you must continue treating similar properties consistently unless facts change significantly.

#### Safe Harbor Requirements (All Must Be Met Annually)
1. **Separate Books and Records**: Maintain separate books/accounts for each RREE (tracking income/expenses). These can be consolidated for tax reporting.
2. **250+ Hours of Rental Services**: 
   - 250 or more hours of "rental services" performed per year on the RREE.
   - For enterprises existing <4 years: 250 hours each year.
   - For longer-existing: 250 hours in at least 3 of the prior 5 tax years (look-back test).
   - Hours count from owners, employees, agents, or independent contractors.
3. **Contemporaneous Records**: Maintain timely logs (e.g., time reports, calendars) detailing:
   - Hours of services.
   - Description of services.
   - Dates services performed.
   - Who performed them.
   - (Required for years after 2018; strict in 2025 audits.)
4. **Signed Statement Attached to Return**: Attach a statement to your timely filed return (e.g., Form 1040, 1065) affirming compliance. If multiple RREEs, list them separately.

#### What Counts as "Rental Services" (Qualifying Hours)
- Advertising to rent properties.
- Negotiating/execution of leases.
- Verifying tenant applications.
- Daily operation, maintenance, and repairs.
- Management/supervision of operations.
- Purchasing materials.
- Collecting rent.
- Providing services to tenants.

#### What Does NOT Count
- Travel time (often miscounted).
- Financing, investing, or arranging loans.
- Studying regulations/financials.
- Long-term improvements/construction.
- Time spent as a tenant or personal use.

#### Exclusions (Cannot Use Safe Harbor If)
- Property used as a residence by you (or related parties) for >14 days or 10% of rental days (Section 280A rules).
- Triple net leases (tenant pays taxes, insurance, maintenance—shifts activity away from landlord).
- Rentals primarily to your own trade/business (unless commonly controlled exception).

#### Relevance to Your Vine Product Rental Plan
Your local rental company (renting out tractors, telescopes, snacks, etc.) is **personal property rentals**, not real estate. This safe harbor applies **only to real property rentals** (e.g., houses, buildings). Equipment/personal property rentals generally qualify as a trade or business more easily under Section 162 (no similar safe harbor needed), so QBI is typically available on net profits without these rules.

If you ever add real estate (e.g., storage units), this safe harbor could apply. For now, focus on documenting active management (daughter's hours) to support trade/business status for personal property rentals.

### Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction – Section 199A (2026 Rules)

The **QBI deduction** allows owners of pass-through businesses (sole proprietors, partnerships, multi-member LLCs taxed as partnerships, S corps) to deduct **up to approximately 23%** of their qualified business income on their personal tax return (Form 1040). It was made **permanent** and enhanced under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), with the standard rate increased from 20% to **23%** starting in tax year 2026.

#### Basic Calculation (2026)
1. **Qualified Business Income (QBI)**: Net profit from your qualified trade or business after deductions (e.g., Section 179 depreciation, expenses) but before the QBI deduction itself.
   - Excludes: Wages you pay yourself, investment income, guaranteed payments (in partnerships).
2. **Tentative Deduction**: 23% × QBI (aggregated across eligible businesses if you elect).
3. **Overall Limit**: Lesser of tentative deduction or 23% of taxable income (minus net capital gains).
4. **New Minimum**: Automatic $400 deduction if you have ≥$1,000 QBI and materially participate (great safety net if phased out).

#### 2026 Income Thresholds (Inflation-Adjusted Estimates)
| Filing Status              | Full 23% Below     | Phase-In Range (Partial Limits)          | Full Limitation Above    |
|----------------------------|--------------------|------------------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Single                     | ~$203,000         | ~$203,000 – $278,000                    | ~$278,000               |
| Married Filing Jointly     | ~$406,000         | ~$406,000 – $556,000                    | ~$556,000               |

- **Below threshold**: Full 23% with **no** W-2 wage or UBIA (unadjusted basis of qualified property) limitations; SSTBs (specified service trades/businesses) treated normally.
- **In phase-in**: Gradual application of wage/UBIA limits and SSTB phase-out.
- **Above threshold**: Deduction limited to the **greater** of:
  - 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, **or**
  - 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of UBIA of qualified property (depreciable assets like your contributed Vine tractors/telescopes—original basis, not depreciated).

#### Is Your Business Eligible?
- **Review Business (TruthSlayer LLC)**: Product reviewing, content creation, affiliate marketing → Generally **not** an SSTB (not reputation/skill-based like consulting/law). Fully eligible.
- **Rental/Leasing Business**: Personal property rentals (tractors, telescopes, equipment) qualify as a trade or business (easier than real estate; no safe harbor needed). Eligible for QBI on net rental income.

3. **Handle Losses**: Negative QBI carries forward indefinitely to offset future positive QBI.
