### **NAICS Code for a Music Record Label**
The most appropriate **NAICS code** for a record label (focused on signing artists, producing, releasing, promoting, and distributing music recordings) is **512250 - Record Production and Distribution**.

- This covers establishments that contract with artists, finance/produce master recordings, and handle promotion/distribution (physical or digital).
- It's under the broader category **5122 - Sound Recording Industries**.

Alternatives if your focus differs:
- **512230** â†’ Music Publishing (if mainly handling copyrights/songwriter royalties).
- **512240** â†’ Sound Recording Studios (if primarily a recording facility).
- **512220** â†’ Integrated Record Production/Distribution (sometimes used for major labels with full vertical integration).

Use **512250** for most independent labels. You can confirm/verify on the official NAICS site or during business registration.

### **Business Structure Recommendations (for EIN and Overall Setup)**
For a new independent music label, the **recommended structure is a Limited Liability Company (LLC)**. Here's why and the options:

- **LLC (Strongly Recommended)**:
  - Provides **personal liability protection** (your home, car, savings are shielded from business lawsuits/debtsâ€”critical in music with potential copyright disputes or artist contracts).
  - Flexible taxation: Defaults to pass-through (like sole prop), but you can elect S-Corp or C-Corp status later for tax savings.
  - Easy to set up and maintain, professional credibility (e.g., "Your Label LLC").
  - Ideal for solo or small teams planning growth, signing artists, or seeking investors.

- **Sole Proprietorship**:
  - Simplest and freeâ€”no filing needed.
  - But **no liability protection** (personal assets at risk) and less professional.
  - Fine for very low-risk hobbies, but not advised for a label dealing with contracts.

- **Corporation (C-Corp or S-Corp)**:
  - More complex/expensive; better for large-scale growth or raising major investment.
  - S-Corp can reduce self-employment taxes once profitable.

Most indie labels start as a **single-member LLC** (if solo) or multi-member (if partners).

#### **For EIN Application (IRS Form SS-4)**
When applying for your free **Employer Identification Number (EIN)** online at irs.gov:
- **Type of Entity**: Select "Limited Liability Company (LLC)".
- **Number of Members**: 1 (single-member) or more.
- **Tax Classification**: Defaults to disregarded entity (taxed as sole prop if single-member), but you can later file Form 2553 for S-Corp election.
- Even sole props can get an EIN (recommended for bank accounts/privacy), but select "Sole Proprietor" if not forming an LLC.

You'll need an EIN for business banking, hiring, or tax filings.

### **Articles of Organization (for LLC Formation with Secretary of State - "SOS")**
"Articles of Organization" (or "Certificate of Formation/Organization" in some states) is the document you file with your state's Secretary of State (SOS) to create the LLC. Costs vary ($50â€“$500+).

Standard required info (no music-specific extras needed):
- LLC name (must include "LLC" or similar; check availability).
- Principal office address.
- Registered agent (person/entity to receive legal docsâ€”can be you or a service).
- Purpose statement: Use general like "any lawful purpose" (most states allow this; no need to specify "music label").
- Management structure: Member-managed (common for small labels) or manager-managed.
- Duration: Perpetual (default).
- Organizer's name/signature.

Some states require more (e.g., member names). File online via your state's SOS website. After approval, draft an **Operating Agreement** (internal rulesâ€”not filed, but essential for clarity on ownership, profits, etc.).

**Pro Tips**:

- Consult a lawyer/accountant for your state specifics (rules vary, e.g., California has extra fees).
- After LLC: Get business licenses if needed, open a business bank account, and join PROs (ASCAP/BMI) for royalties.

### **Multi-Member LLC with Passive Members ("Passive LLC")**

A **multi-member LLC** (2+ owners/members) defaults to the same formation and tax rules as any multi-member LLCâ€”having **passive members** (investors who don't actively manage or participate in operations) doesn't create a separate "passive LLC" category with the IRS or most states. "Passive" refers to how members' income is treated for **self-employment taxes** and **passive activity loss rules** (e.g., limited partners in some structures avoid SE tax), but it's defined in your **Operating Agreement**, not at formation.

This setup works great for a music label: one or more active members run operations (signing artists, releases, marketing), while passive members provide funding and share profits/losses without day-to-day involvement.

#### **NAICS Code (Unchanged)**
Stick with **512250 - Record Production and Distribution**.  
This covers active record label activities (producing, promoting, distributing recordings).  
Even with passive investors, the LLC's primary business is the label's operationsâ€”not pure investment holding.  
If it ever shifts to purely holding royalties/investments (no active production), reconsider **512230 (Music Publishers)** or investment codesâ€”but that's rare for starting labels.

#### **Business Structure & EIN Application**
- **Structure**: Multi-member LLC (default federal tax classification: **Partnership**).  
  - The IRS automatically treats any LLC with 2+ members as a partnership unless you elect otherwise (via Form 8832 for C-Corp or Form 2553 for S-Corp).  
  - Passive members don't change thisâ€”it's still a partnership for filing (Form 1065 informational return; members get K-1s and report on personal taxes).  
  - Benefits: Pass-through taxation (no double tax), flexibility for profit splits not tied to ownership.

- **EIN Application (irs.gov online)**:  
  - Select **Limited Liability Company (LLC)**.  
  - Number of members: 2 or more.  
  - The system will note: "multi-member LLC â†’ classified as a partnership."  
  - Responsible party: Usually the primary/active member.  
  - No special "passive" optionâ€”it's standard multi-member.

- **Tax Implications for Passive Members**:  
  - In a standard member-managed LLC, all members' shares are usually subject to self-employment tax (15.3% on net earnings).  
  - To treat passive members' income as non-SE (like limited partners): Structure as **manager-managed** in your Operating Agreement, with passive members having limited roles (no management/voting rights).  
  - IRS proposed rules (still relied on) allow non-SE treatment if the member: works <500 hours/year, has no binding authority, and limited liability.  
  - Consult a tax proâ€”many labels elect S-Corp status later to minimize SE taxes via salaries/distributions.

#### **Articles of Organization (Secretary of State Filing)**
No changes needed for passive membersâ€”these are public formation docs and don't detail internal roles.  
Standard requirements (varies slightly by state):  
- LLC name (with "LLC" or similar).  
- Principal office/address.  
- Registered agent (you or a service).  
- Purpose: "Any lawful purpose" (fine for a music labelâ€”no need to specify).  
- Management: **Member-managed** (default; all members have authority) **OR** **Manager-managed** (recommended for passive setupâ€”appoint active member(s) as manager(s)).  
  - Choose manager-managed to clearly separate passive investors.  
- Members: Most states **do not require listing member names** publicly (great for privacy). If yours does (e.g., some like NY), list them but note roles elsewhere.  
- Organizer signature.

File online/via your state's SOS site. Cost: $50â€“$500.

**Key Next Step: Operating Agreement (Private, Not Filed)**  
This is where you define "passive" vs. active:  
- Ownership percentages, capital contributions.  
- Management: Manager-managed; name active managers.  
- Voting rights: Limit passive members (e.g., major decisions only).  
- Profit/loss distributions (can be unequal).  
- Passive protections: No day-to-day duties, limited liability emphasis.  
- Buyout/exit rules for investors.  
Use a template (e.g., multi-member manager-managed) and customize with a lawyerâ€”critical for investor attraction and tax treatment.

### Sample Operating Agreement Clauses for Your Multi-Member Manager-Managed LLC (Music Label)

Below are **sample clauses** tailored for a multi-member, **manager-managed LLC** with passive members (e.g., investors funding the label while active members handle operations like signing artists, releases, and marketing). #### 1. Management Structure (Key for Passive Members)
This establishes manager-managed structure, limiting passive members' involvement.

**Sample Clause**:
"The Company shall be manager-managed. The Managers shall have full and exclusive authority to manage and control the day-to-day business, affairs, and properties of the Company, including but not limited to signing artist agreements, entering into distribution contracts, and handling marketing and promotions. Non-managing Members (Passive Members) shall not participate in the management or control of the Company's business and shall have no authority to bind the Company or act on its behalf. The initial Manager(s) shall be [Name(s) of Active Member(s)]. Managers may be removed or replaced by a vote of [e.g., majority/super-majority] of the Membership Interests."

#### 2. Members and Ownership Percentages

**Sample Clause**:

Protects passive investors on big issues while keeping day-to-day with managers.

#### 6. Transfer of Membership Interests / Buy-Sell Provisions

**Sample Clause**:

#### 7. Intellectual Property (Tailored for Music Label)

**Sample Clause**:

#### 8. Dissolution and Winding Up

**Sample Clause**:
"The Company shall dissolve upon [e.g., vote of 75% Interests, bankruptcy, or court order]. Upon dissolution, assets shall be used first to pay creditors, then distributed to Members in proportion to their positive Capital Accounts."

#### 9. Indemnification and Liability
Standard protection.
