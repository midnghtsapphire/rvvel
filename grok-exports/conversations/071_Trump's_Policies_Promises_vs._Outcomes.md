Trump’s 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) significantly altered the tax landscape, reducing the corporate tax rate from 35% to 21%. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) reported that the effective tax rate for the 296 largest, consistently profitable U.S. corporations fell from 22.0% to 12.8% post-2018, resulting in $240 billion less in taxes paid from 2018 to 2021 compared to pre-TCJA rates. This shift favored corporations, with industry-specific data showing significant reductions, such as electronics dropping from 31.0% to 9.3%.

For individuals, the TCJA cut rates, but the Tax Policy Center (TPC) analysis (https://taxpolicycenter.org/publications/distributional-analysis-conference-agreement-tax-cuts-and-jobs-act) indicates these cuts were skewed towards high-income earners. In 2025, the top 1% received an average tax cut of $60,000, while the bottom 60% saw less than $500, with after-tax income boosts of 2.9% for the top 1% versus 0.9% for the bottom 60%. This made the tax system less progressive, with corporate cuts costing $1.9 trillion over 10 years, and individual/estate cuts potentially costing $400 billion annually from 2027.

#### ICE Enforcement: Civil vs. Criminal Detainees, Wrongful Arrests, and Agent Crimes

ICE statistics (https://www.ice.gov/statistics) as of June 15, 2025, show 40,433 out of 56,397 detainees (71.7%) had no criminal record, indicating a focus on civil immigration violations. Arrests include both administrative (civil) and criminal categories, with historical data showing arrests often for DUI, drug possession, and assault.

A GAO report (https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-21-487) found between FY 2015 and Q2 FY 2020, ICE erroneously deported at least 70 US citizens, arrested 674, and detained 121, with at least 895 detainers issued, 74% cancelled. Specific ICE-agent crimes were not detailed in searches, but reports of misconduct, such as using ruses, exist, adding to controversy around enforcement practices.

#### ICE and Bounty Hunters: Disappeared Individuals

There’s no direct evidence of ICE using bounty hunters, but a 2021 ACLU lawsuit alleged illegal use of private contractors like G4S for arrests since 2016, raising legal concerns. Reports indicate individuals “disappeared” by ICE, with the ACLU noting 48 New Mexico residents in March 2025 whose whereabouts were unknown, highlighting human rights issues (https://www.aclu.org/news/immigrants-rights/urgent-human-rights-complaint-submitted-after-ice-disappears-48-new-mexico-residents/).
