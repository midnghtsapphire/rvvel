### Recession Announcements vs. Evidence: Timing, Lags, and Current Snapshot (as of December 13, 2025)

Yes, recessions can—and frequently do—manifest through mounting evidence (e.g., rising unemployment, contracting GDP, or leading indicators like consumer spending dips) **months before** the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) officially announces one. This creates a deliberate lag to avoid false alarms, but it means the economy could be in contraction while policymakers and markets react to real-time signals.

- **Average lead time**: Economic downturns become evident 3-6 months before NBER's dated start, but announcements trail by 8-12 months on average.

### Inverted Yield Curve: Signals, Historical Reliability, and Current Status (December 13, 2025)

An **inverted yield curve**—where short-term Treasury yields (e.g., 2-year) exceed long-term ones (e.g., 10-year)—is one of the most watched economic indicators, historically signaling investor expectations of slowing growth, tighter monetary policy, and an impending recession. It reflects a "flight to safety" in longer bonds amid pessimism about future inflation and expansion. The classic "rule of thumb" from the New York Fed and Cleveland Fed models: Inversions predict a U.S. recession within 6-24 months with ~80-90% accuracy since the 1950s.

#### What It Signals
- **Primary Warning**: Economic contraction. Short rates rise due to Fed hikes combating inflation, while long rates fall on growth fears. This "unwinding" of the normal upward-sloping curve (long > short) has preceded every U.S. recession since 1955, often by 7-18 months.
- **Mechanism**: Banks borrow short-term cheaply but lend long-term expensively, curbing credit/loans and amplifying slowdowns.
- **Not Foolproof**: False positives are rare (e.g., 1966 mini-crisis), but duration matters—prolonged inversions (>6 months) boost hit rates to 100% post-1970.

#### Historical Performance
- **Accuracy**: 87.5% in predicting recessions (YCharts analysis, 1955-2025). Key episodes:
  - 2000 Inversion: Led 2001 recession by 8 months.
  - 2006-07: Preceded 2008 crisis by 12 months.
  - 2019: Signaled 2020 COVID recession (accelerated by pandemic).
- **Recent Track Record**: The 2022-23 inversion (deepest since 1981) raised alarms, but Fed cuts and fiscal stimulus delayed impacts—curve un-inverted by mid-2025, averting a "hard landing."

#### Current Status (as of December 13, 2025)
- **Not Inverted**: The 10-year minus 2-year Treasury spread is positive at ~0.58% (10Y yield: 4.14%; 2Y: 3.56% as of Dec 5). The curve has steepened since Q3 2025, signaling renewed growth confidence post-Fed easing (funds rate at 3.75-4.00% after cuts).

- **Implications**: No signal of imminent recession; instead, it echoes resilient consumer spending. However, watch for re-inversion if trade tensions or inflation rebound.

### OECD Composite Leading Indicator (CLI): Methodology Details

The OECD Composite Leading Indicator (CLI) is a monthly, forward-looking index designed to anticipate turning points in the business cycle (e.g., peaks and troughs) relative to an economy's long-term trend. It provides a broad overview of economic activity, particularly production outlooks, and is calculated for OECD countries, G20 nations, Spain, and major aggregates like the OECD Area. The CLI is non-parametric (no specific economic model assumed) and robust to data revisions. Below is a summary of its construction methodology, based on OECD's standard system (last major updates in methodology documentation around 2009-2010, with ongoing refinements for data availability and quality as of 2025).

#### 1. **Selection of Components**
   - **Pre-Selection Criteria**: Components are chosen from a wide pool of monthly economic series (typically 5-10 per country) that are logically and empirically leading indicators of the reference series (usually Industrial Production Index or GDP proxy). Key filters:
     - **Reference Series Alignment**: Series must lead the economy's cyclical turning points by 6-12 months on average.
     - **Cyclical Conformity**: High correlation with the reference cycle; tested via cross-correlation at optimal lags.
     - **Diversity**: Covers key sectors (e.g., orders, confidence surveys, yields, prices) to avoid over-reliance on one area.
     - **Availability and Quality**: Monthly data preferred; excludes volatile or lagged series. Examples for U.S. CLI:
       - New orders (durable goods).
       - Stock prices (S&P 500).
       - Interest rate spreads (10Y-3M Treasury).
       - Consumer/manufacturer confidence.
       - Building permits.
     - **Evaluation**: Univariate analysis identifies cycles; missing/extra turning points are minimized (e.g., aim for <10% errors over historical data).

#### 2. **Normalization Process**
   - **Step-by-Step Normalization**:
     1. **Seasonal Adjustment**: Using X-13 ARIMA-SEATS to remove seasonal patterns.
     2. **Outlier Detection and Treatment**: Winsorizing or imputation for anomalies.
     3. **Cycle Identification**: Extract cyclical component via filters (e.g., Hodrick-Prescott (HP), Christiano-Fitzgerald (CF), or Phase-Average Trend (PAT)) to isolate deviations from trend.
     4. **Amplitude Adjustment**: Scale components to match the reference cycle's amplitude (standard deviation normalized to 1).
     5. **Lag-Shifting**: Align phases by shifting series to maximize lead (e.g., 4-8 months).
     6. **Inversion (if needed)**: Flip signs for counter-cyclical series (e.g., unemployment).
     7. **Trend Restoration**: Re-add the long-term trend to the cyclical component for the "trend-restored CLI" variant.
   - **Key Formula for Cyclical Component Extraction** (using HP filter example):
     \[
     y_t = \tau_t + c_t
     \]
     Where \( y_t \) is the observed series, \( \tau_t \) is the trend (minimized via penalization: \( \min \sum (y_t - \tau_t)^2 + \lambda \sum (\Delta^2 \tau_t)^2 \), λ=1,600 for quarterly data), and \( c_t \) is the cycle.
   - **12-Month Rate of Change**: Often applied to growth-rate CLI for GDP forecasting.

#### 3. **Aggregation Methods**
   - **Weighting**: Equal weights initially, adjusted via principal component analysis (PCA) or optimization to maximize correlation with the reference cycle at the optimal lead (e.g., 6 months). Weights sum to 1.
   - **Aggregation Formula**:
     \[
     CLI_t = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i \cdot z_{i,t}
     \]
     Where \( w_i \) are weights, \( z_{i,t} \) are normalized components (z-scores or amplitude-adjusted cycles).
   - **Variants**:
     - **Headline CLI**: Raw composite (centered at 100, trend=100).
     - **Trend-Restored CLI**: Adds back trend for level interpretation.
     - **Growth Rate CLI**: 6-month or 12-month % change, used for quantitative forecasts.
   - **Software**: OECD's CACIS (Composite Aggregator and Cycle Identifier System) automates construction and quality checks.

#### 4. **Use for Recession Prediction**
   - **Turning-Point Signals**: CLI deviations below trend (e.g., <99.5) indicate downside risks; sustained drops (>6 months) predict recessions with ~80% accuracy (historical backtests).
     - **Lead Time**: 4-12 months to NBER-dated peaks/troughs.
     - **Probability Forecasts**: Thresholds (e.g., CLI growth <0%) for binary predictions; integrated with probit models.
   - **Quantitative Forecasting**: 
     - Univariate (ARIMA on CLI) or multivariate (VAR with CLI and reference series) for GDP/IIP projections.
     - Example: 12-month growth rate CLI forecasts YoY GDP with RMSE ~1.2% (OECD evaluations).
   - **Performance Metrics**:
     - **Lead Stability**: Mean lead 6-8 months; peak correlation 0.7-0.9.
     - **Revision Analysis**: Small biases (e.g., <0.2% for HP filter); stable signals post-1 year.
     - Historical Examples: CLI signaled 2008 downturn 7 months early (CLI to 98.5 in 2007); 2020 drop to 95.3 preceded by 1 month due to pandemic speed.

#### Recent Updates (as of 2025)
- **Data Enhancements**: Incorporation of high-frequency nowcasts (e.g., weekly sentiment) and AI-adjusted weights for better real-time leads (post-2020 refinements).
- **Coverage Expansion**: Extended to non-OECD G20 (e.g., Brazil CLI with 15 turning points, 53% correlation).
- **COVID Adjustments**: Temporary exclusion of volatile series; focus on post-pandemic resilience signals.
- No major methodological overhaul since 2010, but annual quality reviews (e.g., 2024 OECD report on CLI revisions).

For full technical notes, see OECD's CLI dataset (data.oecd.org) or the 2009 methodology paper by Gyorgy Gyomai. This system ensures CLI's robustness as a non-optimizing benchmark for global cycle monitoring.
