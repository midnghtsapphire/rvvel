## Message 4 - ASSISTANT

The **trapdoor/hatch** in the photo from Jeffrey Epstein's Little St. James island is almost certainly the access point to a **rainwater cistern** (also called a **rainwater harvesting tank** or simply **cistern**). This is a very common system in the **U.S. Virgin Islands** (USVI) and many other Caribbean islands where fresh water is scarce and there's no widespread municipal water supply.

### What other houses/places use this method?
This rainwater collection and storage system is extremely widespread in water-scarce regions, especially islands:
- **U.S. Virgin Islands** — Over **90%** of homes rely on rainwater harvesting via cisterns. USVI building codes require new homes (not connected to public water) to include a cistern, often built underground or integrated into the foundation.
- **Other Caribbean islands** — Places like Bermuda (famous for white stepped roofs designed specifically to channel rainwater into underground tanks), Antigua & Barbuda, Bahamas, Turks and Caicos, and many others mandate or heavily rely on cisterns for residential water.
- **Private islands and remote homes** — Common on small private islands, off-grid properties, or luxury estates in the Caribbean (similar to Little St. James).
- **Other regions** — Parts of Texas, Hawaii, Florida Keys, and arid areas worldwide use cisterns for rainwater harvesting, though often smaller or supplemental rather than primary.

In the USVI specifically, an estimated 90–95% of households use cisterns for their main water supply.

### Is that their running water, shower, etc.? Clothes?
Yes — the cistern typically provides **all** the household's running water in these setups:
- **Showers, baths, toilets** — Standard use.
- **Washing clothes** (via washing machine).
- **Dishwashing, cooking** (after proper treatment).
- **Drinking** — Many people drink it after filtration/treatment, though health guidelines recommend treating it (e.g., boiling, UV, chlorination, or filters) because it can pick up contaminants like bacteria (E. coli has been found in some untreated samples), debris, or animal droppings from the roof.

In non-connected homes, this is the primary (often only) source. During dry spells, owners may buy supplemental water delivered by truck and pumped into the cistern.

### How does it work? What's it called?
It's called a **rainwater harvesting system** or **rainwater catchment system**, with the storage tank specifically named a **cistern**.

**Step-by-step how it works**:
1. **Collection** — Rain falls on the roof (the "catchment surface"). Gutters and downspouts channel the water down.
2. **Initial filtering** — Screens or "first-flush" diverters remove leaves, dirt, bird droppings, and initial dirty runoff (the "first flush" of rain is often diverted away).
3. **Storage** — Water flows into the large underground (or sometimes above-ground) cistern — usually a concrete tank built into or under the house foundation, lined with waterproof material to prevent leaks. Capacities range from thousands to tens of thousands of gallons, sized based on roof area, rainfall, and household needs (e.g., USVI codes often require ~400 liters per square meter of roof).
4. **Access** — A sealed hatch, panel, or trapdoor (like in the photo) allows entry for inspection, cleaning, maintenance, or adding truck-delivered water. It's often in a utility room or floor level.
5. **Distribution** — A pump (electric or sometimes gravity-fed) draws water from the cistern and pushes it through pipes to faucets, showers, etc., just like regular plumbing.
6. **Treatment/Maintenance** — For safety, many add filters, UV lights, or chemicals. Cisterns need periodic cleaning (draining, scrubbing, re-coating) to remove sediment and prevent bacteria/mosquito breeding.

This is a practical, self-sufficient method in places without rivers, lakes, or reliable groundwater. The Epstein island version was likely just a larger/more elaborate take on the same standard system used in countless Caribbean homes — not something exotic or sinister. Fact-checks on the photo confirm it's consistent with a cistern access hatch, not a sea tunnel.

**No, Little St. James was not fully off-grid** — at least not after the early 2000s. While many private islands and homes in the U.S. Virgin Islands (USVI) operate partially or fully off-grid due to limited infrastructure, Epstein's island had **significant grid connections** for power and relied on a mix of systems for water.

### Electricity/Power
- Initially (when Epstein bought it in 1998), the island used **diesel generators** for power, which was expensive — reports mention tens of thousands of dollars per month in fuel costs.
- In **2005**, Epstein paid the Virgin Islands Water and Power Authority (**WAPA**, the public utility) to install a **submarine (undersea) hybrid cable** from St. Thomas to Little St. James. This provided dedicated **electricity** and **fiber-optic data/internet** connections.
- This cable connected the island to the **USVI public grid** (which is powered mainly by fuel oil/diesel plants), eliminating the need for constant on-island generator use. It was a custom, costly setup (Epstein reportedly influenced the cable specs for fiber optics), but it meant the island was **grid-tied** for electricity rather than fully off-grid.
- Backup generators were likely still present for redundancy, as is common on remote properties.

### Water Supply
- The island had a **private desalination system** (reverse osmosis to turn seawater into fresh water) from at least the late 1990s, which supplemented other sources.
- **Rainwater cisterns** (like the one with the trapdoor hatch in the photo) were also used — standard for USVI homes — to collect roof runoff for non-potable or treated uses (showers, laundry, etc.).
- There was no public water line connection (unlike some nearby areas); water came from desalination, cisterns, and possibly occasional trucked-in deliveries during dry periods.
- This is a hybrid setup: not purely off-grid (desalination requires power, which was grid-connected), but self-reliant in collection/storage.

In summary, Little St. James was more **semi-off-grid** or **self-sufficient** in design — especially for water — but **not truly off-grid** after the 2005 cable installation. It combined grid power, desalination, and rainwater harvesting to support the luxury estate and staff/guest needs. This is typical for upscale private islands in the Caribbean: they invest heavily in infrastructure to avoid full isolation while still handling local limitations like scarce freshwater. The cistern hatch fits right into that practical utility setup.

**Details on the WAPA submarine cable to Little St. James**

The **Virgin Islands Water and Power Authority (WAPA)** installed a **submarine (undersea) hybrid cable** connecting Little St. James to St. Thomas around 2005 (with planning and approvals starting as early as 2003).

- **Purpose**: It provided dedicated **electricity** and **fiber-optic data/internet** to the island.
- **Key facts**:
  - Epstein's company (LSJ LLC) bore the full cost of permitting, installation, interconnection, and termination — described as expensive (one report notes he influenced the specs to include fiber optics in a hybrid cable, rather than a standard one).
  - The cable was a **three-core copper conductor** rated at 15 kV for power, combined with fiber optics for data.
  - It ran from Red Hook (on St. Thomas) to Little St. James, roughly parallel to existing lines (e.g., to St. John), at depths up to about 100 feet.
  - Length estimates vary, but similar projects in the area (e.g., related cables) were around 18,000 feet (~3.4 miles).
  - This replaced or supplemented reliance on costly on-island **diesel generators** (previously tens of thousands per month in fuel).
- **Outcome**: It made the island grid-tied for power (via the USVI public grid, which WAPA manages) and gave reliable high-speed internet/data, eliminating generator dependency for primary use while likely keeping backups.
- Sources include contemporary local reports (St. John Source, St. Thomas Source, Virgin Islands Daily News) and Wikipedia summaries citing those.

This was a custom, privately funded project — not standard public extension — highlighting how wealthy owners could invest in infrastructure for remote properties.
